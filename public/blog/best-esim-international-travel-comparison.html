What Is an eSIM and Why You Probably Want One

An eSIM is a digital SIM card built into your phone. Instead of buying a physical SIM at the airport (long queue, ID required, unfamiliar packaging), you scan a QR code or download a profile in the provider's app. Five minutes after landing, you're online with local data.

For travelers, the benefits are huge:

  • No physical SIM swap: Keep your home SIM active for OTPs and calls while running travel data on the eSIM
  • Buy before you fly: Set up the eSIM at home, activate it the moment you land
  • Compare plans on the spot: Need more data mid-trip? Buy a top-up in 60 seconds — no shop visits
  • Multi-country plans: One eSIM can cover an entire region (Europe, Asia, etc.)

The catch: your phone needs to support eSIM. Most phones from 2019 onwards do — iPhone XS and newer, Pixel 3 and newer, and most Samsung Galaxy S20 and later. Some phones bought in China have eSIM disabled by the carrier.

How to Install an eSIM (Step by Step)

The process is the same for all three providers and takes under five minutes:

  1. Buy the plan on the provider's website or app before your trip. You'll get a QR code via email and the app.
  2. On iPhone: Settings → Mobile Data → Add eSIM → Use QR Code → Scan the QR code from another device or screenshot.
  3. On Android: Settings → Network & Internet → SIMs → Add eSIM → Scan QR code (steps vary slightly by manufacturer).
  4. Label your eSIM something memorable like "Travel Yesim" so it's easy to switch between.
  5. Set the eSIM as your default for data only — keep your home SIM for calls and SMS so you still receive OTPs.
  6. Don't activate the plan until you land — most plans have a validity window that starts the moment you turn on data.
Common mistake: Don't delete your home SIM or eSIM. Just disable data on it while traveling. You still want it active for incoming calls, banking OTPs, and verification codes.

5 Common eSIM Mistakes Travelers Make

  • Activating the plan before you land: Some plans expire from the moment you turn on data — even at home. Wait until you land.
  • Not checking phone compatibility: Some Asian-market phones (China, India sometimes) have eSIM locked by the carrier. Test by trying to add an eSIM in your settings before your trip.
  • Buying too little data: 1GB sounds like a lot but disappears in 2 days of normal use. If in doubt, go bigger or pick unlimited.
  • Forgetting to disable data on your home SIM: If both SIMs have data on, your phone might use the home one and rack up roaming charges. Always disable data on the home SIM while traveling.
  • Relying on airport WiFi for setup: Some airports have terrible WiFi or require local SMS verification. Set up your eSIM before you fly.

Frequently Asked Questions

Will my phone number work with an eSIM?

Travel eSIMs are data-only. They don't give you a phone number for calls or SMS in the destination. Your home phone number stays active on your physical SIM (or home eSIM) — you'll still receive OTPs and calls as long as you're not on roaming-disabled mode.

Can I use an eSIM and physical SIM at the same time?

Yes, on most modern phones (iPhone XS and newer, most Android flagships from 2020+). This is called dual SIM mode. Your home SIM stays active for calls/SMS while your eSIM handles data abroad.

Are eSIMs cheaper than buying a local SIM at the airport?

For trips under 2-3 weeks, eSIMs are usually cheaper or comparable. For longer trips (1+ month), local SIMs are often 30-50% cheaper for the same data. eSIMs always win on convenience — no airport queue, no language barrier, instant setup.

Do eSIMs work in China?

Yes, but with caveats. Chinese-market phones often have eSIM locked. International eSIMs from Yesim, Airalo, and Holafly all work in China — and many include built-in VPN routing so you can access blocked apps like Google, Instagram, and WhatsApp. Yesim's free built-in VPN makes it the easiest pick for China travel.

What happens if I run out of data mid-trip?

All three providers let you top up via their app in 60 seconds — no need to buy a new eSIM. Just open the app, pick a top-up amount, pay, and you're back online. This is one of the biggest advantages over physical SIMs.

Is "unlimited" really unlimited?

Not truly. All "unlimited" eSIM plans have a fair-use policy. Yesim throttles to 512kbps after 1-3GB of high-speed daily use. Holafly's caps vary by destination. After throttling, basic browsing and messaging still work, but video streaming becomes painful. For genuinely heavy daily use, no eSIM is truly unlimited.
