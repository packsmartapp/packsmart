Planning Your Costa Rican Adventure

Costa Rica—the "Rich Coast"—delivers rainforests, pristine beaches, zip-lines, wildlife encounters, and adventure sports in a compact package. This Central American gem attracts travelers from the US, UK, Australia, and Canada with its combination of natural wonders, accessible infrastructure, and remarkably affordable adventures. Yet packing for Costa Rica requires thoughtful preparation: jungle conditions demand different gear than beaches, and adventure activities (zip-lining, whitewater rafting, hiking) require specific equipment.

This comprehensive guide covers what to pack for rainforests and beaches, adventure gear for activities, weather-appropriate clothing, entry requirements for Western travelers, region-specific recommendations, and sustainable travel tips. Whether you're exploring Arenal's volcanic landscapes, Manuel Antonio's beaches, Monteverde's misty cloud forests, or the Caribbean coast's laid-back vibes, you'll find practical packing guidance organized by activity and region.

Entry Requirements for US, UK, AU & CA Travelers

Costa Rica offers visa-free entry for most Western travelers, making it an accessible destination. However, requirements vary by nationality and you must meet specific conditions.

United States Citizens

  • Visa requirement: None for tourism (visa-free for 90 days)
  • Passport validity: Minimum 6 months beyond intended departure date
  • Entry documentation: Valid passport and return ticket
  • Cost: Free entry, no visa fees
  • Additional note: Border officials may ask about return travel plans and accommodation details

United Kingdom Citizens

  • Visa requirement: None for tourism (visa-free for 30 days)
  • Passport validity: Minimum 6 months validity
  • Entry documentation: Valid passport and return ticket
  • Cost: Free entry
  • Note: UK citizens receive shorter stays (30 days) than US citizens (90 days); plan extensions if needed

Australian Citizens

  • Visa requirement: None for tourism (visa-free for 30 days)
  • Passport validity: Minimum 6 months validity
  • Entry documentation: Valid Australian passport and return ticket
  • Cost: Free entry
  • Travel insurance: Highly recommended; Australian citizens often benefit from comprehensive travel insurance for medical emergencies

Canadian Citizens

  • Visa requirement: None for tourism (visa-free for 180 days)
  • Passport validity: Minimum 6 months validity
  • Entry documentation: Valid passport and return ticket
  • Cost: Free entry
  • Note: Canada receives the longest visa-free stay (180 days), allowing extended exploration
Pro Tip: Regardless of nationality, obtain travel insurance. Costa Rica has excellent medical facilities, but treatment costs without insurance can be expensive. SafetyWing's travel medical insurance costs as little as $1.50/day and covers emergencies. Keep copies of your passport and travel documents separately from originals.

Best Time to Visit Costa Rica

Costa Rica's weather divides into two distinct seasons, each offering different advantages and challenges.

Dry Season (December-April): Prime Time

  • Weather: Sunny, warm, and minimal rainfall (best hiking and outdoor activities)
  • Temperatures: 75-85°F (24-29°C) depending on region
  • Advantages: Peak visibility for volcano viewing, optimal conditions for zip-lining, clear skies for photography
  • Disadvantages: Peak tourist season with higher prices (hotels $100-300/night), crowded attractions, more expensive flights
  • Best for: Adventure activities, wildlife photography, hiking, beach exploration
  • Packing focus: Sun protection (high-SPF sunscreen, hat, sunglasses), lightweight clothing

Green Season (May-November): Budget & Lush Landscapes

  • Weather: Afternoon rain showers (usually 1-2 hours), morning sunshine, incredible vegetation
  • Temperatures: 75-88°F (24-31°C), more humid than dry season
  • Advantages: Lower prices (hotels $50-150/night), fewer tourists, lush green scenery, excellent wildlife viewing (breeding season), less crowded national parks
  • Disadvantages: Unpredictable afternoon storms (though usually brief), higher humidity, occasional all-day rain, some roads less accessible
  • Best for: Budget travelers, bird watchers, nature photography (dramatic cloud formations), solo travelers
  • Packing focus: Rain gear, quick-dry clothing, waterproof bags, insect repellent

Shoulder Seasons

  • October & early November: Late Green Season with improving weather, still affordable
  • April & May: Transition months; minimal rain, moderate prices, fewer crowds

Essential Costa Rica Clothing by Season

Rainforest & Jungle Clothing (All Seasons)

Costa Rica's rainforests demand specific gear regardless of season. Even in dry season, morning humidity and sudden weather changes require adaptability.

  • Long-sleeve lightweight shirts (2-3): Sun and insect protection. Quick-dry synthetic blends work better than cotton.
  • Lightweight pants or convertible zip-off pants (2): Long pants protect from insects, sun, and plants. Zip-off designs convert to shorts for hot afternoons.
  • Shorts (2-3 pairs): Neutral colors for daytime activities around lodges and towns.
  • Quick-dry t-shirts (4-5): Synthetic blends dry within 1-2 hours despite humidity.
  • Lightweight rain jacket: Packable, breathable, NOT a poncho (ponchos get caught on zip-line equipment). Look for ventilation panels to reduce sweat buildup.
  • Quick-dry underwear: Humidity makes standard cotton problematic. Synthetic materials dry faster and resist mildew.
  • Lightweight sports bra (women): Moisture-wicking design essential in humidity.
  • Socks (3-4 pairs): Wool blend socks dry faster than pure cotton and resist smell in humid conditions.

Beach & Coastal Clothing

  • Swimsuits (2-3): Rotate to ensure one is always dry (humidity prevents quick drying). Consider rash guards for extended water time and UV protection.
  • Lightweight beach dresses or kaftans (1-2): Quick cover-ups that work for beach bars and casual dinners.
  • Linen or cotton shorts (1-2): White or light colors stay cooler; they won't get waterlogged like regular fabrics.
  • Lightweight blouses (1-2): For evening dinners or air-conditioned restaurants.

Evening & Casual Dining

  • Simple sundress or lightweight pants with blouse (1-2): Costa Rica is casual; fancy resort wear rarely needed. Nice casual works for upscale restaurants in San José or tourist areas.
  • Lightweight cardigan or thin wrap: Air-conditioned establishments can be cold; thin layers essential.

Adventure Activity Gear: Zip-Lining, Hiking & Water Sports

Zip-Lining Essentials

Costa Rica's famous canopy tours demand specific gear for safety and comfort. Most operators provide harnesses, but bring:

  • Closed-toe athletic shoes with good grip: Not hiking boots (too heavy), not sandals (dangerous). Trail runners or cross-training shoes work perfectly. Bring lightweight, quick-dry options.
  • Lightweight, breathable long sleeves or rashguard: Protects arms from branches and straps; allows mobility. Short sleeves risk arm scratches.
  • Lightweight pants or capris, NOT shorts: Your legs will be exposed to sun and branches. Quick-dry pants are essential.
  • Water bottle: Stays with you during tours; dehydration is real at elevation with physical exertion.
  • Small daypack: For personal items, sunscreen, phone in waterproof case. Most operators require minimal gear.
  • Avoid: Loose jewelry, sunglasses with lanyards, hats (easily lost while zip-lining), GoPros mounted on heads (creates safety hazards)

Hiking Gear

Costa Rica offers hiking from easy nature walks to strenuous volcano climbs. Pack for your specific trek:

  • Proper hiking boots with ankle support: Trails are muddy, uneven, and slippery in Green Season. Ankle support prevents injuries on roots and rocks. Break boots in before arrival.
  • Quick-dry hiking socks (merino wool blend): 2-3 pairs; they'll be wet constantly in humid, potentially rainy conditions.
  • Lightweight hiking pants or leggings: Long pants protect from insects and plants. Avoid denim (heavy, takes forever to dry).
  • Moisture-wicking undershirts: Cotton absorbs sweat and mildew develops in humidity. Synthetic or merino wool essential.
  • Lightweight backpack (20-30L): For day hikes with water, snacks, sunscreen, rain jacket, camera.
  • Trekking poles: Optional but help on steep volcanic trails and muddy sections. Collapsible poles pack small.
  • Gaiters: Optional; prevent mud and insects from entering boot tops on wet trails.
  • Waterproof dry bag: Protects camera, phone, passport on potentially wet hikes.

Whitewater Rafting & Water Activities

  • Lightweight, non-cotton athletic wear: You will get wet. Quick-dry materials essential.
  • Water shoes or aqua socks: Rivers are rocky; sandals slip off. Water shoes stay on during rapids.
  • Waterproof dry bag with phone: Operators provide them, but bring a backup for personal items.
  • Rashguard or lightweight long-sleeve top: Sun exposure during hours on water is intense. UV protection essential.
  • Avoid: Expensive watches, rings, earrings (loss risk in rapids)
  • Secure your prescription glasses: Bring a sports strap if you wear glasses; loss in rapids is permanent.

Snorkeling & Diving

  • Wetsuit or rash guard: Caribbean water (75-80°F) is warm but protection reduces sun exposure and jellyfish/sea urchin risk.
  • Reef-safe sunscreen: Protect marine ecosystems; chemical sunscreen harms coral.
  • Own snorkel gear (optional): Rentals available but bring your own if prone to ear infections (hygiene).
  • Waterproof camera or GoPro: Capture incredible marine life; remember reef-safe approach (no flash, no touching).
  • Fins/flippers: Rentals usually included; bring your own if you have specific size/comfort needs.

Rain Gear & Waterproof Protection

Proper rain gear distinguishes comfortable trips from miserable ones. Green Season afternoon showers are brief but intense; pack smartly.

Essential Rain Gear (USD)

  • Packable lightweight rain jacket ($30-60): Best choice; packs to fist-size, provides protection, doesn't overheat. Avoid ponchos (catch on zip-line harnesses and equipment).
  • Waterproof backpack cover ($15-25): Protects your pack contents when caught in downpours.
  • Waterproof dry bags (medium & large, $20-40): Two sizes: small for phone/wallet, large for clothes and electronics. Essential in Green Season.
  • Compact umbrella ($10-20): Optional; rain jackets often better for active travel, but useful for stationary activities (waiting at restaurants).
  • Quick-dry pants (2 pairs, $40-70): Avoid denim entirely. Quick-dry fabrics like nylon blends dry in 1-2 hours vs. 12+ hours for cotton.
  • Waterproof phone case ($15-25): Even brief rain can damage phones; essential for outdoor adventures.

Rain Gear Strategy

  • Pack rain jacket in carry-on: Not in checked luggage; you'll need it immediately upon arrival.
  • Invest in quick-dry base layers: Standard thermal underwear absorbs moisture and mildews. Quick-dry synthetics stay fresh.
  • Bring ziplock bags: Plastic bags protect electronics during unexpected downpours. Pack multiple sizes.
  • Accept wet clothes: In Green Season, everything gets slightly damp from humidity. Quick-dry fabrics return to dry within hours. Cotton stays damp for 12+ hours, encouraging mildew.

Regional Packing Guide

Arenal & La Fortuna (Northern Zone)

Highlights: Arenal Volcano, hot springs, rainforest, adventure sports, zip-lining

Climate: Hot, humid, frequent rain (especially Sept-Nov). Mornings cooler and foggier. Afternoon temperatures 80-85°F (27-29°C).

Pack specifically:

  • Heavy-duty rain jacket (very frequent rain)
  • Waterproof bags (electronics at risk)
  • Hiking boots (muddy trails common)
  • Zip-lining gear (this region is ground zero for canopy tours)
  • Hot spring swimsuit and cover-up
  • Insect repellent (mosquitoes active year-round)
  • Long sleeves and pants (protect from insects in rainforest)

Manuel Antonio (Central Pacific)

Highlights: Beaches, rainforest meets ocean, monkeys, sloths, diverse wildlife, hiking

Climate: Hot and humid year-round; Green Season (May-Nov) has afternoon rain. Dry Season (Dec-April) nearly perfect beach weather.

Pack specifically:

  • Multiple swimsuits (you'll spend significant time in water)
  • Beach cover-ups and lightweight dresses
  • Waterproof phone case (water activities, jungle humidity)
  • Hiking boots (national park trails muddy, even in dry season)
  • Quality sunscreen (beach sun intense)
  • Binoculars (wildlife watching essential experience)
  • Quick-dry pants for jungle hikes

Monteverde Cloud Forest (Northern Highlands)

Highlights: Misty cloud forest, unique ecosystem, zip-lining, hiking, bird watching, cool temperatures

Climate: Significantly cooler than lowlands (50-65°F / 10-18°C). Constant mist and frequent rain. You'll need warm layers despite Costa Rica's reputation for heat.

Pack specifically:

  • Fleece jacket or lightweight sweater (essential; it's cold!)
  • Long pants (mist and moisture require leg coverage)
  • Waterproof rain jacket (frequent drizzle)
  • Waterproof hiking boots (muddy, slippery trails)
  • Moisture-wicking base layers (cold + humidity = discomfort)
  • Hat or headband (mist drips constantly from trees)
  • Binoculars (quetzals and exotic birds; bring 10x42 for wildlife)

Caribbean Coast (Limón Province)

Highlights: Laid-back vibe, pristine beaches, coral reefs, Creole culture, chocolate farms, less tourist infrastructure

Climate: Tropical; rain more common than Pacific side. Hot, humid, less developed. Temperatures 75-85°F (24-29°C) year-round.

Pack specifically:

  • Reef-safe sunscreen (coral reefs at risk)
  • Lightweight, breathable clothing (humidity high)
  • Rain gear (more rain than dry season elsewhere)
  • Casual, sustainable fashion (less resort wear, more bohemian vibe)
  • Waterproof bag (humidity and water activities)
  • Comfortable walking sandals (less touristy infrastructure; more walking)
  • Snorkel gear or plan to rent (excellent coral reefs)

Guanacaste Province (Northwest, Including Santa Rosa & Tamarindo)

Highlights: Driest region, beaches, national parks, wildlife, adventure sports, most reliably sunny

Climate: Driest region of Costa Rica. Dry Season (Dec-April) reliably sunny. Green Season still has some rain but less than other regions.

Pack specifically:

  • Extra-strong sunscreen (sun intensity high due to dryness)
  • Wide-brimmed hat (midday sun intense)
  • Lightweight, loose-fitting clothing (sun, heat)
  • UV-protection sunglasses
  • Minimal rain gear (most reliable dry region)
  • Water bottle (stay hydrated in heat)
  • Beach resort wear (touristy areas)

Wildlife Viewing & Photography Essentials

Costa Rica's megafauna (sloths, monkeys, macaws, toucans, jaguars, tapirs, poison dart frogs) represents one of the world's most biodiverse concentrations. Pack smart for respectful wildlife interaction.

Wildlife Viewing Gear (USD)

  • Binoculars (10x42, $100-300): Critical for spotting animals in treetops without disturbing them. 10x magnification with 42mm objective lens is the sweet spot for wildlife.
  • Wildlife field guide (printed or digital, $15-30): Identify species, learn behaviors, understand conservation status. Digital guides on tablets reduce weight.
  • Telephoto lens (200mm+ for DSLR or mirrorless, $200-800): For serious wildlife photography; rent if you don't own one ($30-50/day).
  • Neutral-colored clothing (tan, khaki, green): Bright colors startle animals. Avoid white, red, orange, neon.
  • Waterproof camera or GoPro ($200-500): For action shots and weather resistance.
  • Portable camera cleaning kit ($10-20): Humidity and dust degrade sensors and lenses; protect equipment.

Responsible Wildlife Viewing Guidelines

  • Observe from distance: Use binoculars and telephoto lenses instead of approaching animals. Disturbed animals abandon territories.
  • No flash photography: Flash damages nocturnal animals' night vision and startles sensitive species.
  • Never feed wildlife: Habituated animals become dangerous and lose natural foraging behaviors.
  • Stay on marked trails: Protect vulnerable ecosystems and prevent getting lost.
  • Early mornings (5-8 AM): Most animal activity occurs in early morning; plan hikes accordingly.
  • Hire local naturalist guides ($50-100/day): They know where to find animals, their behaviors, and ecology. Their expertise transforms experiences.

Sun & Bug Protection Essentials

Sun Protection (USD)

  • Broad-spectrum sunscreen SPF 50+ (reef-safe, 200ml, $15-25): Bring MORE than you think you'll need. Caribbean and Pacific sun is intense; reapply every 1-2 hours when in water. Reef-safe formulas protect marine ecosystems.
  • SPF lip balm (2-3, $8-12): Lips burn easily; keep one in pocket, one in backpack, one in beach bag.
  • SPF moisturizer with zinc oxide (daily use, $15-20): For face; easier than reapplying sunscreen constantly.
  • Aloe vera gel (200ml, $8-15): Even with protection, minor burns happen. Refrigerate for soothing relief (some hotels have fridges).
  • Wide-brimmed hat or visor ($20-40): Packable styles fold without damage. Blocks rays without constant reapplication.
  • UV-blocking sunglasses (UV 400, $30-100): Beach, water, and pavement reflect intense UV; eye protection essential. Polarized lenses reduce water glare.
  • Lightweight long-sleeve rashguard (swim shirt, $20-40): For extended water time without sunscreen reapplication.
  • Quick-dry beach sarong or cover-up ($20-35): Protects shoulders from sun while traveling between beach and restaurants.

Insect Protection (USD)

  • DEET-based insect repellent 20-30% ($8-15): Most effective for mosquitoes (dengue, Zika). Apply to exposed skin and clothing perimeter. Reapply after swimming.
  • Picaridin-based repellent 20% (alternative, $10-15): Similar effectiveness to DEET, less greasy, less skin irritation for sensitive folks.
  • Insect repellent wipes ($8-12): Convenient for reapplication during activities; pack multiple sachets.
  • Bug spray for accommodations ($5-10): Spray room perimeter before entering. Check for mosquitoes behind furniture.
  • Mosquito head net ($10-15): Optional but lifesaving in humid, buggy areas (especially La Fortuna). Surprisingly lightweight.
  • Permethrin for clothing/gear ($12-18): Treat hiking clothes and tent (if camping) before departure. Repels insects, withstands multiple washes.
  • Anti-itch cream for bites ($8-12): Hydrocortisone cream provides quick relief from mosquito and sand fly bites.

Health, Safety & Medications

Medications & Health Supplies (USD)

  • Antimalarial medication (if advised, $30-80): Consult your doctor; malaria risk is minimal in Costa Rica but exists in some lowland areas. Arenal and Caribbean coast carry lowest risk; Pacific side nearly zero.
  • Anti-diarrhea medication ($10-15): Dietary changes and unfamiliar water can trigger upset stomach. Keep on hand but avoid overuse (interferes with natural detoxification).
  • Electrolyte packets ($8-12): Dehydration is real in tropical heat; electrolytes replace salt loss from sweating.
  • Pain relievers & anti-inflammatory (ibuprofen 200mg, $5-10): For muscle soreness from hiking and adventure activities.
  • Antihistamine tablets ($8-12): For unexpected allergic reactions or severe insect bites.
  • Antacid tablets ($8-12): Rich, unfamiliar food in humidity can trigger acid reflux.
  • Blister treatment ($10-15): Hiking new trails = new blisters. Pack moleskin, blister pads, and antibiotic ointment.
  • First aid kit essentials ($15-25): Bandages, antibiotic ointment, pain relief, gauze pads. Lodges have basics, but being self-sufficient reduces stress.
  • Prescription medications (full supply + extra days): Bring originals in original containers. Customs requires proof of prescription.
  • Allergy medication (if applicable): Tree pollen and mold count high in rainforests.

Health & Safety Tips

  • Tap water safety: San José and tourist areas have drinkable tap water. Rural and remote areas: stick to bottled water. Hotels typically provide filtered water.
  • Travel insurance essential: Even with free entry, medical emergencies cost thousands without coverage. SafetyWing costs $1.50-2.50/day; excellent value.
  • Mosquito-borne illness risk: Dengue and Zika transmission possible but low in 2026. Use repellent in evening (peak mosquito hours dawn and dusk). Arenal and Caribbean coast carry higher risk than Pacific.
  • Ocean safety: Rip currents exist; ask locals before swimming unfamiliar beaches. Wear reef shoes on rocky/reef beaches (sea urchins and sharp coral).
  • Wildlife safety: Maintain distance (binoculars for viewing). Snakes exist but rarely approach humans. Wear boots and stay on trails.
  • Altitude adjustment: Monteverde (4,700 ft) causes mild altitude effects for some. Ascend gradually; drink extra water.

Toiletries & Personal Care

Travel-Size Toiletries (Bring from Home USD)

  • Shampoo and conditioner (50ml travel sizes, $3-6): Hard water in some areas can leave residue. Bring preferred products.
  • Body wash (50ml, $2-4): Lighter than bar soap; works in any water temperature.
  • Facial cleanser (30ml, $3-5): Humidity, sweat, and sun exposure increase acne; gentle cleanser essential.
  • Toothbrush and toothpaste: Toothpaste tablets (compact) save space.
  • Deodorant (solid preferred, $3-4): Solid takes less space than spray. Antiperspirant reduces sweat in humidity.
  • Feminine hygiene products (if applicable): Available in supermarkets but expensive in remote areas. Bring sufficient supply.
  • Dry shampoo ($4-6): Extends time between washes; helps in humidity when you can't access hair care.
  • Moisturizer with SPF (30ml, $8-12): Replace sunscreen as daily moisturizer; simplifies routine.
  • After-sun moisturizer or aloe vera ($8-15): Hydrates sun-exposed skin; essential even with sunscreen.
  • Razors or shaving cream ($5-10): Available but expensive; bring preferred products.
  • Medications (prescription, original containers): Birth control, asthma inhalers, etc. Declare on customs if asked.

Electronics & Charging

  • Universal power adapter (Type A & B plugs, $15-25): Costa Rica uses 110V with US-style outlets. Most hotels support devices.
  • Portable power bank (20,000+ mAh, $30-50): Heat drains batteries rapidly. Large capacity ensures full-day adventure coverage.
  • Phone charging cable (bring backup, $10-15): Keep one in daypack separate from main bag.
  • Waterproof phone case ($15-25): Essential for water activities and humidity. Test before departure to ensure proper seal.
  • Camera (optional but encouraged, $100-500+): Phone cameras adequate; dedicated cameras offer better wildlife and low-light performance.
  • eSIM or international phone plan ($20-80): Saily eSIM offers coverage in Costa Rica at reasonable rates. Alternative: buy local SIM card upon arrival (Kolbi, Movistar offer prepaid plans).

Sustainable & Eco-Friendly Travel Practices

Costa Rica's conservation ethic—"pura vida"—emphasizes sustainability. Pack accordingly to minimize environmental impact.

Sustainable Packing Choices (USD)

  • Reef-safe sunscreen (mandatory, $15-25): Oxybenzone and octinoxate chemicals harm coral. Zinc oxide or titanium dioxide formulas are reef-safe. Non-negotiable for beach destinations.
  • Reusable water bottle (bring from home or buy there, $15-30): Costa Rica has excellent tap water infrastructure. Refill repeatedly; skip bottled water entirely.
  • Reusable shopping bags ($5-15): Bring lightweight tote. Costa Rica banned plastic bags; support this initiative.
  • Bamboo or reusable utensils ($10-15): For takeout; reduces plastic waste from disposable utensils.
  • Solid toiletry bars (shampoo, conditioner, soap, $8-15 each): Reduce liquid waste and weight. TSA-approved for carry-on.
  • Biodegradable soap ($8-12): If doing laundry or washing clothes in rivers. Regular soap harms aquatic ecosystems.
  • Eco-friendly insect repellent (plant-based, $10-18): Effective alternatives to DEET for low-risk situations. Less effective but greener for casual use.
  • Sustainable clothing (secondhand/ethical brands): Pack less by choosing versatile pieces. Donate worn items to local charities (many lodges coordinate donations).

Sustainable Travel Practices

  • Use certified tour operators: Look for "Certification for Sustainable Tourism" (CST) rating. These companies follow environmental and social responsibility standards.
  • Hire local guides: Direct income supports conservation. Guides earn $50-100/day; tourism revenue directly funds protected areas.
  • Support local businesses: Eat at local restaurants, buy from local artisans. Avoid large international chains when possible.
  • Respect wildlife boundaries: Observe from distance; never touch animals or enter restricted areas. Your restraint preserves ecosystems.
  • Choose small eco-lodges over large resorts: Smaller operations minimize infrastructure impact and often contribute directly to conservation.
  • Offset carbon flights: Use services like Offset.earth to compensate for flight emissions (~$15-30 per flight).

What NOT to Pack for Costa Rica

  • Excessive shoes: Pack 4-5 pairs maximum. You need hiking boots, casual shoes, water shoes, and sandals. Beyond that is excess weight.
  • Formal or dressy clothing: Costa Rica is casual. Flip-flops and t-shirts work for 90% of dining. Nice casual (clean shorts + nice shirt) handles upscale restaurants.
  • Heavy cotton clothing: Humidity makes cotton uncomfortable. Cotton takes 12+ hours to dry; quick-dry synthetics dry in 1-2 hours.
  • Too many toiletries: Buy specialized items (specific shampoos, niche products) at your destination. Standard items available at Caja and Farmacia locations.
  • Expensive jewelry: Beaches, buses, and crowded areas are theft risk zones. Leave watches, necklaces, and rings at home.
  • Guidebooks (physical): Tablet or phone with offline maps and travel apps are lighter. Download detailed guides before departure.
  • Multiple pairs of jeans: Denim is heavy, takes forever to dry, and uncomfortably hot. Avoid entirely.
  • Poncho-style rain gear: Gets caught on zip-line harnesses and backpack straps. Fitted rain jackets are superior.
  • Expensive electronics: Travel with devices you're comfortable potentially losing/damaging. Waterproof protection is good, not guaranteed.
  • Medications without prescriptions: Some medications available over-the-counter in Costa Rica are restricted in your home country. Carry prescriptions and originals only.

Packing Strategy for Costa Rica

  • Roll, don't fold: Rolling saves 30% space and reduces wrinkles. Essential for carryon-only travel.
  • Use packing cubes: Organize by activity (adventure, beach, cool-weather layers). Enables quick outfit assembly.
  • Wear bulky items on plane: Travel in your hiking boots and rain jacket to save luggage space.
  • Pack ziplock bags: Multiple sizes for wet clothes, electronics, first aid, wet swimsuits. Essential in humid, rainy environments.
  • Carry-on only possible: Most 2-week trips fit in a single 40L backpack. Saves baggage fees and reduces lost luggage risk.
  • Use compression bags for bulky items: Lightweight jackets and rain gear compress significantly but don't compress electronics (can damage).
  • Layer dressing: Costa Rica's microclimates vary; dress in layers you can adjust (remove/add) throughout the day.
  • Arrive with minimal luggage: You might buy sandals, additional clothes, or adventure gear in-country. Leave room for return purchases.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I need a visa to visit Costa Rica?
US, UK, Australian, and Canadian citizens do not need a visa to enter Costa Rica for tourism. US citizens receive 90 days; UK citizens receive 30 days; Australians receive 30 days; Canadians receive 180 days. You must have a valid passport with at least 6 months validity beyond your stay. Consider travel insurance even without visa requirements.
What is the best time to visit Costa Rica?
Costa Rica has two seasons: Green Season (May-November) with occasional rain, lower prices, and lush landscapes; and Dry Season (December-April) with more sunshine and higher tourism. December-March is peak season with ideal weather. Green season (June-August and September-November) offers fewer crowds and wet afternoons but lower accommodation costs. Choose based on your preference for weather vs. crowds.
What rain gear should I pack for Costa Rica?
Pack a lightweight, packable rain jacket (not a poncho which gets tangled on zip-lines), waterproof bag for electronics, waterproof dry bag for backpacks, and compact umbrella. Bring quick-dry pants (avoid denim). In rainforest areas, rain comes in short afternoon bursts, so gear that packs small is ideal. Quick-dry fabrics dry within 1-2 hours even in humidity.
What should I pack for wildlife viewing in Costa Rica?
Bring binoculars (10x42 size is ideal), a lightweight field guide to Costa Rican animals, neutral-colored clothing (avoid bright colors that scare animals), and a good camera with zoom capability. Early mornings offer best wildlife viewing. Wear long sleeves in rainforests for sun protection and insect defense. Bring zoom lenses to photograph animals from safe distances without disturbing them.
